Distribution summaries

We can also handle distributions by creating some single-number summaries of the distributions.

If P(x)P(x)P(x) is a distribution function, here are a few commonly encountered summaries:

NameSymbolDescriptionExpression
Meanmean()\text{mean}()mean()Average (or mean) value
Also known as 'expected value' or 'expectation'		∑xP(x)⋅x\sum_x P(x)\cdot x∑x​P(x)⋅x
Variancevar()\text{var}()var()Average squared distance to mean
where μ=mean(X)\mu = \text{mean}(X)μ=mean(X)		∑xP(x)⋅(x−μ)2\sum_x P(x)\cdot (x-\mu)^2∑x​P(x)⋅(x−μ)2
EntropyH()H()H()The average negative log probability
interpreted as the average amount of 'information' in a value of XXX		−∑xP(x)ln⁡(P(x))-\sum_x P(x) \ln\left(P(x)\right)−∑x​P(x)ln(P(x))

What you'll note about these summaries is that they all have the same form. We are forming an average of some quantity over all possible values of XXX, with the average weighted by the probability. The quantity averaged is either:

  • The value itself, leading to the mean or average value
  • The (squared) distance of the value to the mean, forming the variance. This is a measure of how 'spread out' or 'uncertain' the distribution is. If most of the likely values are close together (i.e. we are relatively certain about the value), the variance will be low, but if the probability mass is spread out a lot then the variance will be high.
  • Or (minus) the logarithm of the probability itself, forming the entropy. This is another measure of how 'uncertain' the distribution is. We won't make much use of it in this course but you may come across it.

Understanding variance​

Variance is a measure of how 'spread out' a distribution is (around its mean). It measures the average squared distance of values to the mean.

If a lot of the mass of distribution is far from the mean (i.e. the distribution is 'spread out'), the variance will be large. While if most of the values are close to the mean, the variance will be small.

note

Why 'squared distance' instead of something else, like 'absolute distance' here? There's nothing to stop you measuring 'spread-outiness' by some other mechanism, like the absolute distance to the mean.

One reason to focus on variance, however, is that it corresponds well to the variation of variables that are normally distributed. And as it turns out, most variables are approximately normally distributed (if they summarise lots of data) so that this is often the natural choice.

Understanding entropy​

We won't make much use of entropy in this course, but here is an explainer in case you are interested.

One way to think of the entropy is like this. Imagine we draw a value X=xX=xX=x from the distribution. Suppose we measure how 'surprising' this value is as the negative logarithm of its probability:

surprisingness(x)=−log⁡P(X=x)\text{surprisingness}(x) = -\log P(X=x)surprisingness(x)=−logP(X=x)

The entropy is just the average surprisingness:

H(X)=∑xP(X=x)⋅surprisingness(x)H(X) = \sum_x P(X=x)\cdot\text{surprisingness}(x)H(X)=x∑​P(X=x)⋅surprisingness(x)

Why is this a sensible definition of "surprisingness"? Well, here is a graph of p↦−log⁡(p)p \mapsto -\log(p)p↦−log(p):

Note that:

  • When ppp is large (close to 111), then −log⁡(p)-\log(p)−log(p) is close to zero. This matches the fact that observing a value that is very likely, is not very surprising.

  • On the other hand when ppp is small (close to 000), then −log⁡(p)-\log(p)−log(p) gets very large - in fact it goes to +∞+\infty+∞ at 000. This matches the fact that observing a value that is very unlikely, is very surprising. (Infinitely surprising if actually had zero probability.)

Although I've called this 'surprisingness' above, another way to think of it is 'average information provided by an observation' If the distribution is already very concentrated on one of a few values - i.e. entropy is low - we don't learn much about XXX by observing it. On the other hand if the distribution is very spread-out - i.e. entropy is high - we learn a lot about XXX by observing it.

The entropy, which is the average of −log⁡(p)-\log(p)−log(p), can therefore be thought of as the average surprisingness of an observation or the average amount of information added by an observation.

Information and uncertainty are two sides of the same coin.
